## 3.1 The Communication Advantage

In 2025, the gap between strong and weak communicators in the workplace is widening — partly because AI has removed the execution bottleneck. Someone who writes clearly and quickly can now produce in 30 minutes what used to take half a day.

But AI doesn't automatically make you a better communicator. It amplifies your thinking. If you're clear about what you want to say and who you're saying it to, AI accelerates that dramatically. If you're vague, AI gives you vague output to edit.

This module focuses on practical workflows — what to type, when to use it, and how to refine the output into something you'd actually send.

## 3.2 Email — The Highest-ROI Use Case

Most professionals spend 2–4 hours per day on email. ChatGPT can cut that time significantly.

### Writing Emails from Scratch

**Template:**
> Write a [type] email to [recipient description]. 
> Context: [what's happening / what happened before]
> Goal: [what you want them to do, feel, or understand]
> Tone: [formal/warm/direct/assertive]
> Length: under [X] words
> Do not: [anything to avoid]

**Example — Following up on a proposal:**
> Write a follow-up email to a potential client who received our software proposal 10 days ago and hasn't responded. We met at a conference in March, had a good conversation about their customer service challenges, and sent a tailored proposal. I don't want to seem pushy but do want to re-engage. Tone: warm and professional. Under 150 words.

**Example — Declining a request:**
> Write a polite but firm email declining a request to speak at a conference. I don't have time and it doesn't align with my priorities this quarter. I want to keep the relationship positive and leave the door open for future opportunities. Keep it under 100 words.

### Replying to Difficult Emails

Paste the email you received, then:
> Here's an email I received. Draft a reply that [addresses the complaint professionally / maintains the relationship / declines firmly but kindly / escalates appropriately]. Context: [add what's relevant]. Tone: [specify].

### Improving Existing Drafts

> Here's an email I've written. Improve it for clarity and impact. Keep my main points but make it more concise and professional. Highlight any phrases that sound weak or unclear.

## 3.3 Reports and Proposals

### Executive Summaries

The executive summary is the most read — and most neglected — part of any business document.

> Write an executive summary for a report recommending [topic]. 
> Key findings: [list 3–5]
> Recommendation: [state it]
> Audience: [C-suite / board / non-technical stakeholders]
> Tone: confident and data-driven
> Length: under 250 words
> Lead with the recommendation, not the background.

### Business Proposals

> I need to write a proposal for [what you're proposing] to [client/stakeholder]. 
> Problem we're solving: [describe]
> Our proposed solution: [describe]
> Key benefits: [list]
> Investment/cost: [if applicable]
> Timeline: [if applicable]
> Create a structured proposal outline, then draft the key sections.

### Status Reports

> Write a weekly project status report for senior stakeholders.
> Project: [name]
> Status: [on track / at risk / delayed]
> Key updates this week: [list]
> Upcoming milestones: [list]
> Risks/issues: [list]
> Keep it to one page. Use clear headers. Professional but not stiff.

## 3.4 Presentations and Decks

### Building a Slide Structure

> I need to present [topic] to [audience] in [X] minutes.
> My goal: [inform / persuade / get approval / update]
> Key points I need to cover: [list]
> Create a slide-by-slide outline with: slide title, 2–3 bullet points per slide, and a note on what visual or data would work.

### Speaker Notes

> Write speaker notes for this slide:
> Title: [slide title]
> Bullets: [paste bullet points]
> My talking points should: expand on the bullets without reading them aloud, add a relevant example or stat, transition smoothly to the next slide. Max 4 sentences per slide.

## 3.5 Meeting Preparation and Follow-Up

### Preparing for High-Stakes Meetings

> I have a [meeting type] with [who] in [X] days. My objectives: [list]. Likely agenda: [if known]. Help me:
> 1. Anticipate the 3 hardest questions I'll be asked and draft strong answers
> 2. Identify what information I should bring
> 3. Suggest how to structure my opening statement

**For negotiation prep:**
> I'm negotiating [topic] with [counterparty]. My position: [X]. Their likely position: [Y]. My priorities: [list]. Prepare me with: key talking points, concessions I can offer, red lines I should hold, and likely objections with responses.

### Meeting Agendas

> Create an agenda for a [X]-minute [type] meeting with [number] participants. 
> Objective: [what needs to happen by the end]
> Topics to cover: [list]
> Decisions to make vs. items just for update: [specify]
> Include time allocations.

### Meeting Recaps

Paste a transcript or your rough notes:
> Summarise this meeting into a clear recap. Include: (1) decisions made, (2) action items with owners and deadlines, (3) unresolved questions, (4) next meeting topics. Format as bullet points under each heading. Ready to send to all attendees.

## 3.6 Internal Communications

### Announcements and Policy Changes

> Write an internal announcement for [topic]. 
> Audience: all employees / specific team
> Key information to convey: [list]
> Changes that affect people directly: [list]
> Tone: [reassuring / energising / clear and factual]
> Anticipate 2–3 concerns employees will have and address them in the announcement.

### Performance Review Writing

**Self-assessment:**
> Help me write my annual self-assessment. 
> My role: [role]
> Key achievements this year: [list]
> Challenges I navigated: [list]
> Areas I'm developing: [list]
> Company values to reference: [list]
> Tone: confident but not boastful. Specific, not vague.

**Writing reviews for direct reports:**
> Help me write a performance review for a team member. Their role: [role]. Strengths observed: [list]. Areas for development: [list]. Key achievements: [list]. Overall rating: [high performer / meeting expectations / needs improvement]. Tone: honest, constructive, and growth-focused.

## 3.7 Tone Adaptation

One of ChatGPT's most underused capabilities is adapting the same content for different audiences.

**The technique:**
Write the core message once, then:
> Rewrite this for [different audience]. Original was written for [original audience]. Adjust the: vocabulary, level of detail, assumed knowledge, and tone.

**Examples:**
- Technical document → non-technical executive summary
- Internal process document → customer-facing explanation
- Formal report → casual team Slack message
- English version → simplified version for non-native speakers

## 3.8 Building Your Communication Template Library

The highest-leverage thing you can do after this module: build a library of your 10–15 most-used communication prompts.

**How to structure it:**
Create a document or Notion page with sections:
- **Emails:** Sales follow-up, complaint response, internal update, meeting request, decline
- **Reports:** Executive summary, status update, post-mortem
- **Meetings:** Agenda, pre-meeting prep, recap
- **Internal comms:** Announcement, performance review, policy change

For each, save a working prompt template with [placeholder] brackets. When you need it, paste the template, fill in the brackets, and send to ChatGPT.

A well-built template library saves 30–60 minutes every working week.

## Key Takeaways

- AI cuts business writing time dramatically — but your thinking about audience and goal still drives quality
- Use the **email template** for any communication: type, recipient, context, goal, tone, length, avoid
- The **executive summary** is the highest-leverage document in any report — use AI to lead with the recommendation
- **Meeting prep prompts** help you anticipate hard questions and enter every meeting better prepared
- **Tone adaptation** — rewriting content for different audiences — is one of ChatGPT's strongest capabilities
- Build a **communication template library** and you'll save hours every week
